Section 01

What Problem Are We Solving?

Imagine you run a popular restaurant. On a normal Tuesday, 30 customers walk in and you comfortably seat them all. But on New Year's Eve, 300 people show up at once. You can't serve everyone — so you need a system: a bouncer at the door who decides who gets in, how fast, and in what order.

In the world of software, APIs (Application Programming Interfaces) are the doors to online services. When you open an app, it sends dozens of tiny requests — "fetch my messages," "check my balance," "load this image" — to remote servers. Those servers can only handle so many requests at once. Without control, they get overwhelmed and crash.

Key Term — API Gateway

An API Gateway is the digital bouncer. It sits in front of a server and controls which incoming requests are allowed through, how many per second, and in what order. This project is about making that bouncer smarter using AI.

The Problem with Old-School Bouncers

Traditional rate limiters use fixed rules — for example: "allow a maximum of 12 requests per second, full stop." These rules work fine on a calm Tuesday, but they struggle when:

Example — Fixed Rules Failing

A shopping website normally gets 100 requests/second. It sets a hard limit of 150. On Black Friday, 5,000 requests arrive per second. The fixed limiter blocks most of them — including urgent payment confirmations — treating them the same as low-priority image loads.


Result: customers see payment failures even though the server could have prioritized those requests and processed them fine.

Old Approach
The Rigid Bouncer

"The list says max 12 people. I don't care if one of them is the CEO having a cardiac event — rules are rules."

This Project's Approach
The Intelligent Bouncer

"12 people is the guideline, but I'll adapt. I see payment requests piling up — I'll let those through first and hold the low-priority ones."

This research project builds that "intelligent bouncer" using a technique from AI called Reinforcement Learning.

Section 02

The AI Brain — Reinforcement Learning & PPO

So how do you teach a computer to make smart admission decisions? You could write thousands of rules by hand. Or you could let the AI learn by doing — which is exactly what Reinforcement Learning (RL) does.

What is Reinforcement Learning?

Think of training a dog. You don't explain the rules in English — you give treats when it does something right and say "no" when it doesn't. Over thousands of repetitions, it figures out what behavior earns treats. Reinforcement Learning is the same idea, but for software. The AI tries actions, gets "rewards" or "penalties," and slowly learns what to do.

The Three Core Concepts

01
State — What does the agent observe?

At every moment, the AI looks at the current situation: how many requests are waiting in the queue, how long they've been waiting, how many were recently dropped, what the current traffic level is, and the priority mix of waiting requests.

02
Action — What can the agent do?

The AI has exactly three choices: decrease the rate limit (let fewer requests in), hold it steady, or increase it (let more in). These change the limit in steps of 2, within the range of 2 to 18 requests per step.

03
Reward — How does it know if it did well?

After each action, the environment scores the outcome. High throughput earns positive points. High latency (slow responses), dropped requests, and especially dropped high-priority requests earn penalties. The AI learns to maximize its total score over time.

Why PPO Specifically?

PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) is a popular, stable RL algorithm. Think of it like a student who learns cautiously — it makes small, measured improvements each time rather than wild swings. This prevents the AI from "forgetting" good strategies while trying new ones.

Example — PPO in Plain Language

Imagine you're learning to ride a bike. One approach: every time you wobble, completely change your grip and posture (unstable learning). PPO's approach: make small corrections each time — lean slightly left, adjust speed a little. Over time, these careful improvements add up to a great rider, without ever falling completely off the bike.

The Training Environment

The AI trained across 260 simulated episodes, each 180 timesteps long. During training, it experienced four realistic traffic patterns:

🌿 Low Load

Traffic below capacity. Like a café on a Monday morning — easy to manage, but the AI must not over-admit and waste resources.

🔥 High Load

Sustained overload. Requests pour in faster than the server can handle. The AI must triage aggressively.

⚡ Bursty

Normal traffic with sudden spikes. Like a flash sale — calm, then chaos, then calm again. The AI must react quickly.

〰️ Oscillating

Traffic rises and falls in a wave pattern. The AI must anticipate cycles rather than just react to them.

Importantly, each request also has a priority class: high (e.g., payment), medium (e.g., profile load), or low (e.g., analytics ping). Dropped high-priority requests earn heavier penalties.

Section 03

The New Idea — Cost-Aware Admission

The original rate limiter treated all requests as equal units: "I can serve 12 requests per step." But in reality, not all requests cost the same to process. A simple lookup query takes milliseconds; a large AI model inference takes seconds and much more compute.

The Insight

This project's key innovation: replace the simple "12 requests per step" limit with a processing budget of 24 "cost units" per step. Each request carries a cost between 1 and 5. The server can process as many requests as fit within that budget.

Example — Cost Budget vs. Fixed Count

Old system (fixed count = 12 per step): Receives 12 image-generation requests, each costing 5 units. Serves all 12 — but total cost = 60 units, way beyond real capacity. Server crashes.


New system (budget = 24 cost units): Receives 12 image-generation requests (cost 5 each). Can only serve 4 of them (4 × 5 = 20 ≤ 24). Also fits one cheap request (cost 3, total = 23). Serves 5 requests total — but server stays healthy.


Or alternatively: 24 cheap requests arrive (cost 1 each). All 24 can be served within budget — much higher throughput!

This means the AI now has a much harder problem. It can't just count requests — it must reason about the mix of costs in the queue and decide admission limits accordingly. Two cheap requests might be preferable to one expensive one, depending on the situation.

New Information the AI Can See

To handle cost awareness, the state was extended with new features the AI can observe:

New State Features — Cost-Aware Signals

• Total queued service cost — how expensive is the entire backlog?
• Mean queued service cost — on average, how costly is each waiting request?
• Queued cost per priority class — are the expensive requests high-priority or low-priority?
• Recent arrival cost — is the incoming traffic getting cheaper or pricier?
• Rejected cost last step — how much useful work did we turn away?

Section 04

The Hardest Part — Getting the Reward Right

This section is the heart of the research. Defining what "good" means for the AI turned out to be the most critical challenge — and getting it wrong caused spectacular failure.

Attempt 1: The Original Count-Based Reward

The basic reward formula rewarded the AI for serving more requests and penalized it for latency, dropped requests, and especially dropped high-priority ones:

reward = + throughput_score
- latency_penalty
- drop_rate_penalty
- high_priority_drop_penalty ← this was set too high (3.1×)
- overload_indicator

The problem: the high-priority penalty was set to 3.1× — extremely aggressive. The AI became so paranoid about ever dropping a high-priority request that it throttled everything down, serving almost nothing. A policy so conservative it defeated its own purpose.

⚠️ What Went Wrong

Imagine a student who loses 31 marks for every wrong answer, but only gains 10 for a right answer. They'd stop answering entirely rather than risk the penalty. The AI did the exact same thing.

Fix: Reducing the high-priority penalty to 1.0× immediately restored healthy behavior. Throughput jumped from 8.6 to 11.7 requests/step, and high-priority success improved from 83% to 90%.

Attempt 2: Naive Cost-Aware Reward (The Collapse)

When the project added cost awareness to the environment, the team naively kept the old "serve more requests = good" incentive and just added a small bonus for serving expensive requests:

reward = + throughput_score (request count)
+ small_cost_bonus   ← the new addition
- penalties...

This failed catastrophically. Here's why:

Example — Misaligned Incentives

The server now measures capacity in cost units, not request counts. But the AI is still being graded mainly on request counts. So when it admits many cheap low-cost requests (costing 1 unit each), it burns through the 24-unit budget serving 20+ requests... but those 20 cheap requests cause congestion, latency spikes, and score poorly on penalties.


The AI discovered that the safest path to minimizing penalties was to stop admitting almost anything. By episode 180, it was serving barely 2 requests per step and had abandoned most of the budget entirely.

Budget Utilization (Naive Reward)~26%
High-Priority Success (Naive Reward)~49–71%
Drop Rate (Naive Reward)~87%

Attempt 3: Cost-Only Reward (The Fix)

The solution was to replace the request-count objective entirely with a cost-served objective. The AI now got positive points only for how much computational work it facilitated — not how many requests it let in:

reward = + served_cost_score ← replaces request count
- latency_penalty
- drop_rate_penalty
- high_priority_drop_penalty
- overload_indicator

Now the AI's goal aligned perfectly with the environment's constraint. Serving a high-cost request felt as rewarding as serving several cheap ones. The policy stabilized immediately, and budget utilization climbed to near 97–98% across most scenarios.

Budget Utilization (Cost-Only Reward)~97%
High-Priority Success~88%
Drop Rate~20–48%
The Central Lesson

In constrained RL systems, reward design is more important than algorithm choice. You can have the best AI algorithm in the world, but if you're rewarding the wrong thing, the agent will find a clever way to game your metric while solving the wrong problem.

Section 05

How Well Did It Work?

After training with the correct cost-only reward, the researchers compared their AI against two types of opponents: a simpler AI (count-blind PPO) and hand-crafted rule-based systems called "heuristics."

Cost-Aware AI vs. Simple AI

Scenario Metric Simple AI Cost-Aware AI Winner
High Load Requests served 12.0 / step 7.8 / step Simple AI
Budget used 100% 97.5% Both good
Bursty Total reward 124.5 164.4 Cost-Aware AI
Budget used 84% 96.6% Cost-Aware AI
Oscillating Total reward 145.5 157.8 Cost-Aware AI
Budget used 91% 97.5% Cost-Aware AI

The cost-aware AI serves fewer raw requests — but that's intentional. It's not under-performing; it's choosing to serve more computationally valuable work within the same budget. Think of it like a chef who makes fewer, more complex dishes rather than churning out simple ones.

How Does It Handle Surprises? (Out-of-Distribution Tests)

The real test of any AI is what happens when conditions change in ways it wasn't trained on:

✓ Cheap Flood

Hundreds of tiny, cheap requests flood in. The AI handled this well — cheap requests pack efficiently into the budget, and it learned to keep utilization high. Reward: +171

✗ Expensive Attack

A few extremely costly requests monopolize the budget. The AI collapsed badly — it never saw costs this high during training and didn't know how to ration them. Reward: −1203

vs. Hand-Crafted Heuristics

The AI was also tested against human-designed rule systems that explicitly encode domain knowledge (e.g., "throttle more when queue cost exceeds X"). The honest conclusion: heuristics often won. The AI remained competitive, but didn't dominate.

Why Heuristics Are Hard to Beat

A hand-crafted rule like "if queued cost > threshold, raise limit" took a human seconds to write but encodes years of domain insight for free. The AI had to discover this relationship from scratch through thousands of trial-and-error interactions — a much harder path.

Section 06

A Real-World Scenario Where This Matters

🏦 Case Study — Digital Banking Platform

A Bank's API Gateway During Peak Trading Hours

Imagine a major bank with a mobile app used by 5 million customers. Their backend API serves three very different types of requests — each with radically different costs and priorities.

🔴 High Priority · High Cost
Payment transactions, loan approvals, fraud checks. Cost: 4–5 units. Cannot drop.
🟡 Medium Priority · Medium Cost
Account balance checks, transaction history. Cost: 2–3 units. Delay is acceptable.
🟢 Low Priority · Low Cost
Push notification delivery, analytics pings, background sync. Cost: 1 unit. Droppable.

The Crisis: Month-End Processing Day

On the last day of the month, salaries are credited simultaneously. 2 million customers open the app at the same time. The gateway receives 50,000 requests per second — 10× normal load. Without smart rate limiting:

Without Smart Rate Limiting — The Disaster

• A fixed limiter lets in everything up to a count of 12,000/sec regardless of cost
• Background analytics floods the queue first (thousands of cheap pings)
• Budget exhausted by low-value work
• Real payment confirmations queue for 40+ seconds
• Customers think transactions failed and retry, making things worse
• Server crashes under retry storm — the bank's busiest day becomes its worst outage

With This Project's Cost-Aware PPO System

01
AI observes the state

Sees: queue growing fast, mean queued cost rising (expensive payment requests piling up), high-priority drop rate spiking. Budget utilization near 100%.

02
AI decreases the rate limit

Reduces overall admissions to protect budget. Low-priority analytics pings get rejected first (they're designed to be retried later anyway).

03
Budget now serves valuable work

The 24-unit budget goes toward payment confirmations (cost 5 each → 4–5 per step) plus some balance checks. Payments process within 2 seconds. Customers are happy.

04
Load normalizes, AI adapts

As the spike subsides, the AI gradually increases the rate limit again, allowing analytics and notifications to resume. No crash, no customer complaints.

The Bottom Line

In a production banking system, this approach could mean the difference between a seamless payday experience and a widely-reported outage. The AI optimizes for computational value delivered, not just a raw request count — which maps directly to business outcomes.

Section 07

Where This Work Falls Short

Any honest research paper must acknowledge what it doesn't solve. Here are the key limitations — some discussed in the paper, some additional ones that apply broadly to this type of work:

Limitation 01
Poor Generalization to Unseen Cost Distributions

The AI was trained on request costs ranging from 1–5. When the "expensive attack" scenario sent costs far outside this range, performance collapsed. It learned the scale of training, not the abstract concept of "cost packing."

Limitation 02
Heuristics Remain Difficult to Beat

Hand-crafted rules that directly encode domain knowledge (e.g., "throttle when queued cost exceeds a threshold") outperformed the learned policy in several stress scenarios. The AI paid a heavy sample-efficiency tax for discovering what an engineer could express in one line.

Limitation 03
Discrete, Limited Action Space

The AI can only choose between three actions: decrease, hold, or increase the rate limit in fixed steps of 2. A real system might benefit from continuous control (e.g., set limit to exactly 7.3) or per-class admission policies, not just one global knob.

Limitation 04
No Online Adaptation

Once trained, the AI's behavior is frozen. If traffic patterns change permanently (a new type of workload, a different cost distribution), the system can't adapt without being retrained from scratch. Real deployments need continuous learning.

Limitation 05
Simulation vs. Reality Gap

The entire system is trained in a simplified simulation. Real networks have packet-level jitter, TCP retransmissions, cascading failures, and hardware variability that the simulator ignores. The policy may behave differently in a real datacenter.

Limitation 06
Single Gateway — No Coordination

The research models one isolated gateway. In practice, large systems have hundreds of gateways, load balancers, and microservices. A policy that works for one node may create new bottlenecks elsewhere if it's not coordinated across the system.

Limitation 07
No Safety Guarantees (SLOs)

The AI optimizes a reward signal, but gives no hard guarantees. In production, systems must meet Service Level Objectives (SLOs) — e.g., "99.9% of payments complete in under 3 seconds." The current approach can't formally enforce such constraints.

Limitation 08
Fine-Tuning Hurts Robustness

When the researchers specialized the model on high-load scenarios, its performance on unusual attack scenarios got worse. This "catastrophic forgetting" is a known problem in neural networks — specializing for one thing often means forgetting something else.

Suggested Future Directions

The paper itself suggests: online adaptation to new cost regimes, multi-gateway coordination, explicit safety constraint layers, richer feature engineering, and combining learned policies with structural priors about how expensive requests should be packed — rather than pure model-free RL.

Summary

Putting It All Together

This project set out to answer one question: can a learning AI manage API traffic better than static rules, and can it reason about the computational cost of each request it serves?

The answer is a qualified yes. The AI successfully learned to:

✓
Use the budget efficiently

Reaching ~97% budget utilization across standard scenarios — far above the naive approach that wasted 74% of available capacity.

✓
Prioritize valuable work

Maintaining ~88% high-priority success rates even under heavy load, by learning to protect important requests.

✓
Generalize to some new situations

Handling the cheap-flood scenario gracefully even though it was never seen during training.

!
But with real limits

Failing on adversarial cost attacks, unable to beat the best hand-crafted heuristics, and with no safety guarantees for production deployment.

The deepest lesson isn't about rate limiting — it's about AI design. What you reward the AI for matters more than which algorithm you use. A misaligned reward silently guides the AI toward the wrong solution, and the failure can look deceptively sensible until you examine it carefully. Getting the reward right is engineering as much as it is art.
